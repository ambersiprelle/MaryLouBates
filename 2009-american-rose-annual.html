Article One

The Gift of Memories

Roses have a way of carrying memories across time. A single bloom can transport you back to a grandmother's garden, a summer afternoon, a person you loved. There is something in the scent of a rose — something ancient and familiar — that reaches past the everyday mind and into the places where our most treasured moments are kept.

When I think about the roses in my life, I think about the people who planted them. The gardens that were tended with care long before I arrived and that continued to grow long after those gardeners were gone. Each rose is a kind of letter from the past — a living message that says: I was here, I loved something beautiful, and I wanted it to live on.

This is the gift of memories. Not photographs or journals, but living things that bloom every year, that fill the air with fragrance, that ask nothing of us except a little water and attention. Roses are patient that way. They outlast the gardener. They outlast the garden. And in their faithfulness, they keep faith with everyone who ever loved them.

I have been lucky to inherit roses — roses with histories, with names, with stories attached to them. Old garden roses whose origins are sometimes uncertain, sometimes disputed, but always fascinating. Each one came to me through a chain of hands: a cutting from a friend's garden, a division from a neighbor's border, a slip taken over the fence on a summer morning. That is how roses travel. That is how memories move through time.

To grow roses is to participate in something much larger than yourself. It is to become one link in a chain that stretches back centuries, and to pass that chain forward to whoever will come next — a child, a neighbor, a stranger who stops to ask the name of that beautiful pink climber on the fence. When you hand them a cutting, you hand them a story. And the story goes on.

Article Two

Mam-Maw Sarah's Yellow Roses

Mam-Maw Sarah had yellow roses. I don't know what variety they were — she never called them by any name except "my yellow roses." But they were magnificent, and they bloomed every June with a reliability that seemed almost like a promise kept.

She grew them along the fence at the back of her property, where they scrambled and arched and tumbled over in long canes that had to be tied back every spring. The flowers were a warm, buttery yellow, full-petaled, with a sweet fragrance that carried all the way to the porch where she spent her evenings. When the wind was right, you could smell them from the road.

I took a cutting from those roses the summer before she passed. I wasn't sure it would survive — I am not always skilled with cuttings — but I wrapped it in damp paper and prayed, and by fall it had rooted. It grew slowly at first, tentatively, as though it wasn't sure of its new home. But by the second spring it had settled in, and the third year it bloomed — the same warm yellow, the same sweet fragrance, the same generous canes reaching for the sky.

Now I have Mam-Maw Sarah's yellow roses in my own garden. They bloom every June, and every June I think of her. The roses don't know they've traveled, don't know the chain of love that brought them here. But I know. And that is enough.

Heirloom roses carry their histories in their genes, in the particular way a petal is shaped, in the exact shade of color that no hybridizer has quite managed to replicate. They are living archives. They outlast the people who loved them. And in doing so, they honor those people in the most faithful way imaginable — by continuing to be exactly what they have always been: beautiful, fragrant, and persistent.

If you have roses that came to you from someone you loved, grow them well. They are more than plants. They are stories. They are proof that love, like a good rose, is perennial.
