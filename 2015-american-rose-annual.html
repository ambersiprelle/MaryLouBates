Feature Article

Roses Take You Away Down Memory Lane

There is a particular quality to memory that roses seem to understand instinctively. A fragrance carries you somewhere before your mind has even caught up — you are already in a garden you haven't visited in twenty years, standing beside someone who is no longer here, feeling the sun on your face on an afternoon that has otherwise been entirely forgotten. Roses do this. They are time machines with petals.

I have been a rose grower for many years now, and I have noticed that the roses I love best are the ones with stories attached to them. Not the prize-winning hybrid teas with their perfect exhibition blooms, though I admire those too — but the old ones, the heirlooms, the ones whose names are sometimes uncertain and whose origins are sometimes guessed at rather than known. These are the roses that carry memory. These are the roses that take you down the lane.

The Old Ones Remember

When I walk through a garden planted with antique roses, I am walking through layers of time. A gallica rose that has been grown in European gardens since the Middle Ages. A China rose that arrived in the West two centuries ago and changed everything that came after. A bourbon rose born of an accidental cross on an island in the Indian Ocean. Each of these has a history longer than any of us can fully imagine, and yet here they are, blooming in our gardens, as alive and fragrant as ever.

There is something humbling about that. Something that puts your own little span of years in proper perspective. A rose that was growing in monastery gardens when Chaucer was alive is not particularly interested in your schedule or your worries. It blooms when it blooms. It rests when it rests. It endures.

The Personal and the Universal

But memory lane, for most of us, is not medieval. It is closer and more personal — a grandmother's garden, a childhood home, a neighbor who pressed cuttings into your hands one autumn afternoon and said, simply, "You'll want this one." These are the memories that roses carry forward, from one garden to the next, one generation to the next.

I have roses in my garden that came from people I loved. When I tend them, I am in some sense still in relationship with those people. The roses don't let the connection break. They insist on it, blooming every year with a faithfulness that puts us humans to shame.

That is what roses do. They take you back to places you thought you'd left. They take you to people you thought you'd lost. They lead you, gently and irresistibly, down memory lane — and they make you glad you went.
